April 2026

April remained a targeted execution month rather than a broad expansion month. Active states largely held their previously posted windows, while several watch states added clearer operational language without opening a new local cycle. The executive takeaway is that the near-term opportunity set is concentrated in a limited number of states with confirmed deadlines or rolling access.

Southeast

Georgia
Georgia remains one of the clearest near-term opportunities in the tracker. The FY2024 application window stays open through April 29, 2026 in Georgia EM Grants Manager, and the state is already signaling a September 1, 2026 opening for FY2025. For leadership, Georgia remains both a current-cycle and next-cycle planning state.
Source
South Carolina
South Carolina continues to present a live FY2024 opportunity with applications routed through SLED. The current cycle opened March 20, 2026 and runs through May 15, 2026. This keeps South Carolina in the active near-term pursuit group.
Source
Alabama
Alabama continues to operate through a first-come, first-served service-delivery model rather than a traditional local cash subaward approach. That makes the access path more operational and partner-driven than a typical competitive grant round. The state remains relevant, but the pursuit motion is different from a standard application window.
Source
Mississippi
Mississippi’s January 15 to February 27, 2026 batch is closed, and the state still says any further Batch 4 opportunity has not yet been determined. The current signal is operational pause rather than reopening. This remains a monitor state until the next batch is formally posted.
Source

Mid-Atlantic

Maryland
Maryland continues to offer one of the more accessible current pathways in the tracker. The state shows an open rolling second-round FY2023 opportunity alongside an additional short-term funding opportunity. From a leadership standpoint, Maryland remains an actionable rolling-access state.
Source
Virginia
Virginia continues to emphasize a committee-led shared-services model rather than a broad open local competition. The state directs entities to VCPC meetings and the VDEM listserv for future SLCGP notifications. This remains a relationship and monitoring state with centralized signaling.
Source
Pennsylvania
Pennsylvania continues to route local participation through Commonwealth-selected projects rather than a direct federal or broadly open application path. The public language remains steady and still emphasizes monitoring and sign-up. This keeps Pennsylvania in a controlled-access posture rather than an open-window posture.
Source

Northeast

Michigan
Michigan now clearly states that FY2022, FY2023, and FY2024 awards are complete. FY2025 timing, including the application date and submission deadline, remains to be determined while Advisory Board review continues. The practical takeaway is clarity on program maturity, but no immediate local opening.
Source
Wisconsin
Wisconsin states that FFY23 round-two applications closed on January 30, 2026 and remain under review. The state is telling stakeholders to watch for the FFY24 NOFO release. This remains a watch state with clearer sequencing but no open local window.
Source
Nebraska
Nebraska’s site continues to show the 2024 subrecipient application as closed while keeping committee members and meeting listings visible. That provides useful governance visibility, but no new dated 2026 local opening is posted. Nebraska remains better for monitoring than immediate pursuit.
Source
Maine
Maine continues to show FY22 and FY23 participation windows closed, with projects underway. The page also references a partial-shutdown service pause affecting the KnowBe4 training component. Leadership should read this as implementation-stage activity rather than a live new-entry opportunity.
Source

West

Idaho
Idaho remains open through an active participation-interest model tied to prospective FY2027 shared services under Operation Cyber Idaho. This is a meaningful access path even though it does not resemble a traditional competitive grant cycle. Idaho remains one of the clearer service-entry states in the West.
Source
Wyoming
Wyoming remains open and continues to post a clear due date of May 15, 2026 through ZoomGrants. That makes it one of the cleaner late-spring deadlines in the tracker. It stays in the active short-list for near-term action.
Source
Oklahoma
Oklahoma continues to post quarterly 2026 deadlines, but the official page also says funding is not currently accessible and additional funding is expected later this year. The practical signal is mixed: there is structure on paper, but not a fully actionable funding path today. Oklahoma remains a monitor state unless access language improves.
Source
Oregon
Oregon remains temporarily closed as of April 1, 2026 and continues to say it anticipates reopening for new applications in summer 2026. That gives leadership a credible future timing signal without a current entry point. Oregon should stay on the summer watch list.
Source
Washington
Washington still does not post a clearly dated 2026 local application window on the reviewed page. The state continues to reference a November 2025 funding update and prior webinar materials, which helps with context but not with current actionability. Washington remains a monitoring state until a dated local cycle is posted.
Source

March 2026

March was defined by formal window announcements, award communications, and clearer direction on next-cycle timing. Several states moved from general monitoring to concrete application or post-award messaging. This was the month when the national picture became more actionable for near-term sales planning.

Southeast

Florida
Florida remains closed on the last posted cycle, which ran from October 15 to December 15, 2025. The state continues to indicate a later 2026 FY24 release. This keeps Florida in the next-cycle watch group rather than the current active group.
Source
North Carolina
North Carolina’s FY2025 cycle remains closed, with awards estimated for spring 2026 or later because of federal shutdown-related delay. The state remains relevant because of the expected award movement, but not because of an open application path. Leadership should treat this as a post-window monitoring state.

West

California
California’s FFY2024 Local and Tribal RFP closed on March 13, 2026. The state is no longer in an open pursuit phase for that cycle. California remains important as a recent close and follow-on monitoring state.
Source
Ohio
Ohio’s Round 3 .gov migration application closed on March 13, 2026. The state capped federal support at $9,500 per project with a 20% cost share, making the scope relatively defined and targeted. This is now a closed, specialized cycle rather than a broad current opportunity.

February 2026

February captured several late-winter close dates and a notable increase in detail from meeting records and state guidance pages. For leadership, the month primarily clarified where opportunities had already closed and where agencies were shifting into review or implementation. It also sharpened the distinction between true application windows and service-delivery models.

Southeast

Louisiana
Louisiana meeting minutes state that the FY23, FY24, and FY25 SLCGP application period ran from January 1 to February 28, 2026. The same materials directed applicants to prioritize mitigation and training. The state is better read as a recent-close environment with clear priority framing.
Source
Mississippi
Mississippi’s official page confirms the 2026 window opened January 15 and closed February 27, 2026, with Batch 4 participation still undetermined. This entry is important because it marks the formal close timing and preserves the state’s open question on the next access point. It remains a monitor state until the next batch is defined.
Source

Northeast

Kansas
Kansas’s January 1 to February 28, 2026 submission window is now paired with language that Phase 2 applications are under committee and federal review. That gives stronger post-submission visibility than many states provide. Kansas is therefore a review-stage state, not an open-window state.
Source
Maine
Maine clarified that Year 1 and Year 2 participation windows are closed and that projects are underway. The page also notes a temporary shutdown-related pause affecting a funded training service. This is a useful implementation signal, but not a current application opening.
Source

January 2026

January established the early-year baseline for the tracker. Activity was concentrated in planning, committee visibility, and the first clearer signals on how states intended to route local participation. Most states were still in watch mode, but several began to show more concrete access pathways.

Northeast

Nebraska
Nebraska’s SLCGP site continues to show the 2024 subrecipient application as closed while keeping committee materials and meeting listings visible. That provides governance transparency but not a new 2026 opening. The state remains best categorized as a structured watch state.
Source
Connecticut
Connecticut continues to route eligible entities through a DEMHS sub-application model. The reviewed page still does not post a newly dated 2026 local window. Connecticut remains structured and monitorable, but not currently open.

West

New Mexico
New Mexico’s FY2027 participation-interest and request-form model remains one of the clearest early-cycle service pathways in the tracker. The state is still notable because the access path is visible even without a traditional open competition. For leadership, New Mexico stays strategically important as an early-entry planning state.
